## Summary

Thailand implemented stricter export controls on crude palm oil (CPO) effective April 7, 2026, requiring all exporters to obtain written approval from the Secretary-General of the Central Price-Fixing Committee under Thailand's Ministry of Commerce before exporting CPO. The measure was prompted by rising global oil prices driven by Middle East conflict and anticipated increased domestic demand for biodiesel blending. No individual or entity may export CPO without explicit written authorization.

## Key Details

- **Issuing Authority**: Thailand Central Price-Fixing Committee, Ministry of Commerce (Domestic Trade Department)
- **Effective Date**: April 7, 2026
- **Scope**: All crude palm oil (CPO) exports from Thailand; applies universally to all individuals and entities

## Key Provisions

- Effective April 7, 2026, all CPO exports require written approval from the Secretary-General of the Central Price-Fixing Committee
- Universal application: no individual or entity is exempt from the prior-approval requirement
- Rationale: Middle East conflict driving global oil price increases; energy sector expected to raise biodiesel blending ratios in diesel fuel
- Rising CPO export demand combined with anticipated domestic supply pressure necessitated protective supply measures
- Administered through Thailand's Ministry of Commerce, Domestic Trade Department

## Supply Chain Impact Assessment

**[Assessment — speculative, not confirmed policy]**

Thailand is one of the world's largest CPO producers and exporters, and this tightening of export controls will directly affect global vegetable oil supply chains, particularly importers in South and Southeast Asia, Europe, and China. The prior-approval requirement adds lead time and administrative complexity for CPO buyers, potentially driving up spot prices in the short term. The biodiesel connection is significant: as Middle East tensions sustain high crude oil prices, demand for palm-based biodiesel feedstock will remain elevated, making this restriction likely to persist. Companies relying on Thai CPO for food manufacturing, oleochemicals, or biofuel production should diversify sourcing toward Indonesian CPO or alternative vegetable oils and build strategic inventory buffers ahead of anticipated tightening.
