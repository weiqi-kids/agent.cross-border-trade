## Summary

On March 31, 2026, Canada finalized an updated version of the Canadian Export Control List (ECL) Guidelines, implementing Canada's commitments under multilateral export control regimes through January 1, 2026. The updated January 2026 version will take effect May 1, 2026, replacing the current May 2025 version. Key changes include the addition of tetramethylguanidine and other items, and the removal of specific tunable lasers and underwater visual systems from the control list.

## Key Details

- **Issuing Authority**: Government of Canada, Export Controls Policy Division (Global Affairs Canada)
- **Effective Date**: May 1, 2026 (new January 2026 version); current May 2025 version valid through April 30, 2026
- **Scope**: Canadian Export Control List (ECL); all exporters using the Export Control Online (EXCOL) system; multilateral regime commitments

## Key Provisions

- March 31, 2026: Canada finalized the January 2026 version of the ECL Guidelines
- New version implements Canada's multilateral export control regime commitments through January 1, 2026
- Export Control Online (EXCOL) system users notified March 31; second notice to be sent by April 30 confirming May 1 effective date
- 30-day familiarization period provided before the new guidelines take effect
- **Additions to control list**: Tetramethylguanidine and other items (specific full list in official guidelines)
- **Removals from control list**: Specific tunable lasers; underwater visual systems
- **Streamlined revision procedures** introduced in the new version
- Contact for inquiries: expctrlpol@international.gc.ca (Export Controls Policy Division)

## Supply Chain Impact Assessment

**[Assessment — speculative, not confirmed policy]**

Canada's update to the ECL reflects ongoing alignment with multilateral export control regimes (Wassenaar Arrangement, Australia Group, etc.) and signals continued tightening of controls on dual-use and strategic items. The addition of tetramethylguanidine—a strong organic base used in pharmaceuticals, agriculture, and as a rocket propellant precursor—is particularly notable, as it suggests increased sensitivity to chemical/precursor dual-use risks. The removal of specific tunable lasers and underwater visual systems may ease compliance burdens for exporters in photonics and marine technology sectors. Companies exporting from Canada in chemicals, advanced materials, and specialized electronics should review their product classifications against the new January 2026 ECL to ensure continued compliance from May 1, 2026. The 30-day familiarization window is tight for complex supply chains requiring reclassification.
