## Summary

The Customs Law of the People's Republic of China establishes the legal framework for customs supervision and management of cross-border movements of goods, transport vehicles, and personal items. Originally enacted in 1987 and amended in 2000 and 2013, it defines customs authorities' powers including inspection, tariff collection, anti-smuggling enforcement, and oversight of prohibited or restricted goods under national law.

## Key Details

- **Issuing Authority**: Standing Committee of the Sixth National People's Congress
- **Effective Date**: 1987-07-01 (originally); amendments on 2000-07-08 and 2013-06-29
- **Scope**: All import and export activities, cross-border transport vehicles, cargo, and personal items entering or leaving PRC territory; customs supervision and enforcement nationwide

## Key Provisions

**Legislative Purpose (Article 1):**
- Safeguard national sovereignty and interests
- Strengthen customs supervision and management
- Facilitate foreign economic, trade, scientific, and cultural exchanges

**Core Customs Responsibilities:**
- Supervision of inbound/outbound transport vehicles, cargo, and items
- Collection of tariffs and other taxes/fees
- Investigation and suppression of smuggling
- Compilation of customs statistics

**Declaration Requirements:**
- Import cargo consignees must declare within 14 days from the transport vehicle's arrival declaration
- Export cargo shippers must declare 24 hours before loading

**Customs Powers:**
- Inspection of transport vehicles and examination of cargo
- Review of contracts, invoices, and related documents
- Detention of suspicious goods
- Inquiry into financial records related to import/export transactions

**Bonded Goods (保税货物):**
- Goods entering the territory without completing tax procedures (subject to approval)
- Can be stored, processed, or assembled domestically and then re-exported

**Prohibited and Restricted Goods (Article 40):**
- Customs implements supervision over prohibited or restricted goods in accordance with laws, administrative regulations, and State Council provisions
- Specific measures to be formulated by General Administration of Customs

**Penalties for Smuggling (Article 82):**
- Confiscation of smuggled goods, items, and illegal proceeds
- Concurrent fines may be imposed
- Criminal liability pursued if conduct constitutes a crime

**Customs Broker Violations (Articles 88-89):**
- Unregistered brokers engaging in customs declaration activities shall be banned
- Illegal agency or scope violations result in fines, suspension, or revocation of registration

**Bribery (Article 90):**
- Revocation of customs declaration registration and professional qualification
- Fines imposed; permanent disqualification if criminal conduct is established

**Enforcement Oversight:**
- Supervisory authorities monitor customs administrative enforcement
- People's Procuratorate oversees anti-smuggling police investigative activities
- Internal separation of declaration review, examination, release, and audit functions

## Supply Chain Impact Assessment

**[Speculative Analysis]**

This foundational law creates the legal backbone for all export control implementation in China. While it does not enumerate specific controlled items, Article 40's framework for "prohibited or restricted goods" supervision provides the statutory basis for subsequent export control regulations on dual-use items, military products, and emerging technologies.

**Potential Supply Chain Implications:**
- **Declaration Timing**: The 24-hour pre-loading declaration requirement for exports may create logistical constraints for time-sensitive shipments, particularly for technology products requiring last-minute configuration changes.
- **Customs Inspection Authority**: Broad powers to examine cargo and review commercial documents mean export control compliance documentation (end-user certificates, license copies) will be subject to routine customs scrutiny.
- **Bonded Processing**: The bonded goods regime (allowing imported materials to be processed and re-exported without full customs clearance) intersects with export control when re-exported finished goods incorporate controlled foreign-origin components.
- **Broker Liability**: Severe penalties for customs brokers (including permanent disqualification for bribery) incentivize strict compliance, potentially leading brokers to refuse handling controlled shipments without comprehensive documentation.
- **Anti-Smuggling Enforcement**: The law's emphasis on combating smuggling, combined with criminal liability provisions, suggests heightened scrutiny of exports in sectors known for diversion risks (semiconductors, chemical precursors, aerospace components).

**Strategic Considerations for Cross-Border Traders:**
- Companies exporting controlled goods from China must ensure their customs brokers are fully informed of export control obligations, as broker errors could trigger both administrative penalties and operational delays.
- The 14-day import declaration window may affect inbound shipments of controlled foreign technology requiring pre-import licensing, necessitating advance coordination with Chinese importers.
- The law's knowledge of intellectual property protection (Article 44) suggests customs may detain exports suspected of containing unlicensed technology, adding another layer of risk for technology transfers.
