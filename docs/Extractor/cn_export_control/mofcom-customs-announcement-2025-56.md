## Summary

China announces comprehensive export controls on rare earth production equipment and raw materials, effective November 8, 2025. The announcement adds 26 categories of rare earth processing equipment (2B902 series) and 3 categories of rare earth raw materials and reagents (1C914 series) to the dual-use items export control list. This represents China's most extensive rare earth export control expansion to date, covering the entire rare earth industrial chain from mining to magnet production. Export permits are required for all listed items under the Export Control Law and Dual-Use Items Export Control Regulations.

---

## Key Details

- **Effective Date:** November 8, 2025
- **Legal Basis:** Export Control Law of the PRC, Foreign Trade Law of the PRC, Customs Law of the PRC, Regulations on Export Control of Dual-Use Items of the PRC
- **Approval Authority:** State Council
- **Implementation Agencies:** MOFCOM (licensing), GACC (customs enforcement)
- **Controlled Items:** 26 categories of rare earth equipment (2B902.a through 2B902.z), 3 categories of rare earth materials (1C914.a through 1C914.c)
- **Scope:** Export controls apply to all outbound shipments of listed items
- **Declarant Requirements:** Exporters must indicate "dual-use items" status and control codes in customs declarations; for items with parameters approaching control thresholds, must declare "not controlled items" with specific parameters

### III. Export Licensing and Customs Declaration Requirements

**Licensing Requirements:**
- Exporters must apply for export permits from MOFCOM in accordance with the Export Control Law and Dual-Use Items Export Control Regulations
- Applications subject to end-user and end-use review

**Customs Declaration Requirements:**
- Exporters are responsible for the authenticity of declared goods
- Must strengthen identification of export items
- **For controlled items:** Must indicate "dual-use items" in the remarks column of customs declarations and list the dual-use item export control code
- **For items with parameters approaching control thresholds but not controlled:** Must indicate "not controlled items" in the remarks column and provide specific parameters, indicators, and performance data
- Customs may challenge declarations with incomplete, inaccurate, or questionable authenticity; goods will not be released during the challenge period

**Synchronous Update:**
- The "Export Control List of Dual-Use Items of the People's Republic of China" will be updated simultaneously with this announcement

## Supply Chain Impact Assessment

**This assessment represents analytical interpretation and should be treated as speculative.**

### Immediate Impacts (November 2025 - Q1 2026)

**Global Rare Earth Equipment Supply:**
- Export of Chinese rare earth processing equipment to international buyers will require MOFCOM permits, potentially creating 30-90 day delays for non-sensitive destinations
- Rare earth processing facility construction projects outside China (particularly in Vietnam, Malaysia, and Africa) may face equipment sourcing delays
- Equipment manufacturers may need to redesign systems to avoid control parameters or seek alternative suppliers

**Raw Material and Chemical Supply:**
- Export of rare earth ores (bastnäsite, monazite, ionic clay) now controlled; affects downstream processors in Japan, South Korea, and Western countries that import Chinese rare earth concentrates
- P507, P204, and other extraction reagents are critical inputs for rare earth separation; non-Chinese rare earth processors (e.g., Lynas, MP Materials) may face supply disruptions if they source these chemicals from China
- Flotation reagent controls may impact ore beneficiation operations outside China

**Magnet Manufacturing Supply Chains:**
- Vacuum sintering furnaces, hydrogen decrepitation systems, and jet mills are specialized equipment often sourced from China; magnet manufacturers outside China (particularly those producing NdFeB magnets for EVs, wind turbines, and defense) may face equipment replacement challenges
- Grain boundary diffusion equipment controls may impact advanced magnet production capabilities outside China

### Medium-Term Impacts (2026-2027)

**Technology Transfer and Localization:**
- Controls on complete rare earth production systems may slow technology transfer to countries attempting to build domestic rare earth industries (Australia, USA, India, EU)
- May accelerate development of alternative technologies or domestic equipment manufacturing capabilities in non-Chinese countries, though at higher cost and longer timelines

**Strategic Stockpiling:**
- Downstream rare earth processors and magnet manufacturers may increase inventory of critical extraction chemicals (P507, P204, N235) and accelerate qualification of alternative suppliers
- Countries with rare earth development projects may prioritize securing equipment supplies before controls fully take effect

**Industry Consolidation:**
- Smaller non-Chinese rare earth processors without access to alternative equipment sources may face viability challenges
- May strengthen China's dominant position in global rare earth processing (currently ~90% of global capacity)

### Long-Term Strategic Implications (2027+)

**Supply Chain Restructuring:**
- May accelerate Western initiatives to develop China-independent rare earth supply chains (e.g., US Defense Production Act investments, EU Critical Raw Materials Act projects)
- However, high capital costs and technical complexity of rare earth processing may limit success of complete decoupling efforts

**Geopolitical Leverage:**
- Enhances China's ability to control global rare earth supply not just through resource dominance but also through technology and equipment gatekeeping
- May be used as leverage in technology disputes, trade negotiations, or geopolitical tensions
- Particular impact on industries critical to energy transition (EV motors, wind turbines) and defense (guidance systems, jet engines, radar)

**Market Dynamics:**
- May increase rare earth prices globally due to supply uncertainty and processing bottlenecks
- Could incentivize development of rare-earth-free technologies (e.g., ferrite magnets for some motor applications, alternative permanent magnet chemistries)

**Compliance and Due Diligence:**
- Equipment manufacturers and chemical suppliers must implement robust export control compliance programs
- Potential for increased regulatory scrutiny and penalties for violations

### Sectors Most Affected

1. **Electric Vehicle Industry:** NdFeB magnet production for EV traction motors
2. **Renewable Energy:** Wind turbine permanent magnet generators
3. **Defense and Aerospace:** High-performance magnets for military applications, rare earth crystals for lasers
4. **Electronics:** Rare earth phosphors, magnets for hard drives and speakers
5. **Industrial Automation:** Servo motors and robotics using rare earth magnets

### Risk Mitigation Considerations

- Companies should conduct immediate inventory of rare earth equipment and chemical dependencies on Chinese suppliers
- Accelerate qualification of alternative suppliers for extraction chemicals (though global supply is limited)
- Evaluate equipment redundancy and maintenance strategies for critical rare earth processing systems
- Engage with governments on strategic stockpiling and domestic industry development programs
- Consider long-term offtake agreements and joint venture arrangements to secure equipment access

## Notes

This announcement represents China's most comprehensive expansion of rare earth export controls to date, covering the entire value chain from mining reagents to finished magnet production equipment. The technical specifications are highly detailed, indicating careful mapping of China's rare earth industrial capabilities. The 26 equipment categories and 3 raw material categories represent strategic industrial knowledge accumulated over decades of rare earth industry development.

The requirement for exporters to declare specific parameters for items "approaching" control thresholds creates additional compliance burden and grants customs authorities broad discretion in determining control applicability. The simultaneous update of the Dual-Use Items Export Control List ensures immediate legal enforceability.
