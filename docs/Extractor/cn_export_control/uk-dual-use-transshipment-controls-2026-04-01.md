## Summary

The UK government has announced plans to introduce new accompanying regulations implementing end-use control sanctions, requiring UK exporters to apply for export licenses when exporting specific goods assessed as high-risk and potentially subject to transshipment to sanctioned destinations, particularly Russia. The announcement was made by UK Minister of State for Trade Chris Bryant in response to a parliamentary question, building on extensive trade sanctions imposed since March 2022 to prevent Russia from obtaining dual-use items and high-risk military technologies.

## Key Details

- **Issuing Authority**: UK Department for Business and Trade (Minister of State for Trade Chris Bryant)
- **Effective Date**: Forthcoming (accompanying regulations to be introduced; no specific date stated)
- **Scope**: UK exporters of dual-use goods; targets high-risk goods susceptible to transshipment to sanctioned destinations (primarily Russia); affects global dual-use trade flows through third-country intermediaries

## Key Provisions

- The UK has implemented extensive trade sanctions since March 2022 targeting Russia's access to dual-use items and high-risk military technologies
- New accompanying regulations will implement **end-use control sanctions**:
  - UK exporters of specific high-risk goods must apply for **export licenses**
  - Trigger: goods assessed as at risk of transshipment to sanctioned destinations (e.g., Russia via third countries)
- Government will provide additional guidance on due diligence obligations for UK exporters
- Policy responds to documented transshipment routes by which sanctioned dual-use items reach Russia through intermediary countries
- The question was raised in Parliament by Labour MP Mark Sewards, indicating cross-party legislative interest

## Supply Chain Impact Assessment

> *The following is an analytical assessment, not confirmed fact.*

- **Transshipment hub risk**: Countries frequently used as Russia-bound transshipment routes (e.g., UAE, Turkey, Kazakhstan, Armenia, Georgia) face increased scrutiny; UK exporters sending goods to these destinations will need enhanced due diligence
- **Compliance burden**: UK exporters of electronics, semiconductors, machinery, and other dual-use goods face additional licensing requirements, increasing compliance costs and lead times
- **China relevance**: Chinese companies exporting to Russia or serving as intermediaries in Russia-bound supply chains may face secondary exposure if UK-origin components are in their products; watch for alignment with EU and U.S. anti-circumvention measures
- **Sanctions evasion risk**: The new end-use controls signal that Western governments are moving from broad sanctions to targeted transshipment interdiction—a trend that will intensify scrutiny of all cross-border dual-use trade
- **Trade flow impact**: Legitimate traders in high-risk categories may need to restructure supply chains or obtain additional certifications to maintain UK market access
